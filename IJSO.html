IJSO

The International Junior Science Olympiad (IJSO) is an annual international competition in science for students under the age of 16. It covers three major fields: physics, chemistry, and biology. IJSO requires interdisciplinary knowledge instead of individual subject, so participant must be able to integrate concepts from all three sciences to solve complex problems. The first IJSO was held in 2004, and now the competition includes participants from over 40 countries worldwide. Usually, IJSO is held in December each year.

Some key features of IJSO

  • Age Limit: participants must be under 16 years old.
  • Three Sciences: physics, chemistry, biology.
  • Exams Format: theoretical, practical, and multiple-choice sections.

Thailand's Representative Selection for IJSO

In Thailand, we have 3 rounds of selection to choose 6 students to represent Thailand in IJSO. The details of selection can be found here. (TH language) Anyway, I made the summary here. Let's say IJSO is held in december of year Y, the registration for selection would start from november of year Y - 1 , and the selection will end in may of year Y. Details of each rounds can be briefly summarized as follows:

Round Months Subjects Formats Full Marks Participants Selected Participants
1 January Math, Phys, Chem, Bio All MCQ 4 Ã— 100 = 400 6000 - 8000 300
2 March Phys, Chem, Bio All Written (Except Bio) 3 Ã— 100 = 300 300 30
3 May Phys, Chem, Bio All Written 3 Ã— 100 = 300 30 6 + 4 (Reserved)

There is a bit more detail about each rounds. For example, if you are quailified in round 2 to be one of 30 finalists, you must attend a training camp (held in april - may) for about 3 weeks before taking exam of round 3. The training camp is very cramping and intense. There is almost no break within those 3 weeks, so be prepared. In addition, if you pass round 3 to be one of 6 + 4 representatives, you must also attend another training camp for about 10 days (held in october) before going to IJSO.

There are so many times when students ask about the cut-off marks for qualification for each rounds. The honest answer is "I don't know". The cut-off marks are different every year, depending on the difficulty of the exam and the performance of participants. However, from my experience:

  • Round 1: if you can do 200 out of 400, you should be safe.
  • Round 2: I recommend that 200 out of 300 is quite debatable.
  • Round 3: I really have no idea, but people would aim the very highest mark, like at least 240 out of 300.
